# TakeMeter: Evaluating Discourse Quality in r/formula1

TakeMeter is a machine learning pipeline that automatically evaluates and classifies fan discourse quality within the Reddit Formula 1 community (`r/formula1`). By sorting community text into structural layers, TakeMeter aims to separate empirical, data-driven engineering evaluation from reactionary background noise and speculative opinions.

---

## 1. Project Overview & Community Context

In Formula 1 communities, the quality of discourse varies dramatically. During a race weekend, a single discussion thread contains a dense, challenging mixture of real-time telemetry analysis, wild career speculation ("hot takes"), and immediate, emotionally charged venting.

To map this ecosystem, TakeMeter classifies text into three mutually exclusive, exhaustive categories grounded in community norms:

1. **`technical_analysis`**: Structured, objective arguments backed by telemetry, lap times, tire degradation matrix parameters, or mechanical physics.
2. **`hot_take`**: Bold, highly confident, speculative opinions about driver skills or team politics lacking objective empirical proof.
3. **`emotional_reaction`**: Immediate, non-analytical emotional outbursts, celebrations, or curses reacting to live track events.

---

## 2. Dataset Distribution & Curation

A dataset of **200 high-fidelity examples** was curated manually from public `r/formula1` post-race and live weekend text streams. The dataset was strictly balanced to prevent majority-class predictive bias:

- **`technical_analysis`**: 62 examples (31.0%)
- **`hot_take`**: 68 examples (34.0%)
- **`emotional_reaction`**: 70 examples (35.0%)
- **Total Corpus**: 200 rows (split 70/15/15 into Train, Validation, and Test sets automatically by the pipeline).

## 4. Error Analysis & Boundary Failures

The confusion matrix highlights a definitive, unidirectional failure vector: **the fine-tuned model misclassified 2 true `technical_analysis` examples as `hot_take`**, resulting in an analysis recall drop to 0.80. Conversely, both models achieved flawless $1.00$ performance across the `emotional_reaction` category.

### Deep-Dive of Specific Fine-Tuned Failure Rows

1. **Failure Example 1: The Complex Diagnostic Failure**
    - **Text**: _"Analyzing the shock absorber displacement logs indicates that Mercedes ran their ride height 3mm too low, causing severe underbody plank grounding on curbs."_
    - **Ground-Truth**: `technical_analysis`
    - **Model Prediction**: `hot_take`
    - **Failure Analysis**: Assisted by pattern analysis tools, we discovered that the model struggles when a post details mechanical failures or performance anomalies without a comparative conclusion. Because the phrase contains the words "severe underbody plank grounding", DistilBERT interpreted "severe" as a marker of hyperbolic hot-take sentiment, missing the rigid engineering framework of "shock absorber displacement logs" and "3mm ride height".

2. **Failure Example 2: The Latent Strategy Predictive Slip**
    - **Text**: _"The strategy simulation software likely failed to project the pit stop traffic window, releasing Hulkenberg directly into a dense DRS train of four lower midfield cars."_
    - **Ground-Truth**: `technical_analysis`
    - **Model Prediction**: `hot_take`
    - **Failure Analysis**: This error occurs due to the speculative hedging word _"likely"_. While the post describes an explicit strategy optimization failure mapped to a specific track position ("dense DRS train"), the word "likely" triggered the model's learned associations for speculation, causing it to override the heavy technical feature space and bucket it as a subjective opinion.

3. **Failure Example 3: Contrastive Baseline Superiority (Why Llama Won)**
    - **Text**: Same text as Failure 1 and 2.
    - **Ground-Truth**: `technical_analysis`
    - **Baseline Prediction**: `technical_analysis`
    - **Failure Analysis**: Llama 3.3 70B possesses an expansive contextual window and massive world-knowledge parameters regarding Formula 1 concepts. It recognizes that "shock absorber displacement logs" is a highly narrow, esoteric engineering metric. DistilBERT, limited by its smaller parameter footprint and a compact 200-example training set, relies heavily on individual lexical indicators like "severe" or "likely", making it prone to boundary slips on complex, technical hybrid texts.

## 6. Conceptual Reflection: Intended vs. Learned Behavior

The project exposed an acute linguistic gap between our conceptual taxonomy and the model's mathematical optimizations. While I intended the model to evaluate the _substantive structural backing_ of a statement, DistilBERT primarily mapped _lexical certainty and stylistic sentiment_.

It successfully clustered pure emotional exclamations due to intense punctuation and capitalization patterns. However, the boundary separating an analytical deduction from an opinionated hot take was flattened down to superficial keyword parsing. If an objective technical post included expressions of doubt ("likely") or high-severity adjectives ("severe"), the model regularly over-indexed on those sentiment markers, failing to realize that the core thesis was fully anchored in telemetry facts.

## 7. AI Tool Usage Log & Disclosures

- **Instance 1 — Pre-Labeling Data Collection Support**: An external large language model was utilized to pre-sort raw extracted text from `r/formula1`. To ensure complete data integrity, a mandatory boolean tracker column (`is_ai_prelabeled`) was appended to the master CSV. Every single row was manually parsed and reviewed by me. I explicitly overrode and corrected approximately 14% of the LLM's assignments where it routinely mislabeled sarcastic technical rants as true `technical_analysis`.
- **Instance 2 — Multi-Row Failure Cluster Profiling**: Post-training, all misclassified rows from the test set were fed into an LLM with the prompt: _"Identify common grammatical or lexical anomalies shared among these misclassified items."_ The AI successfully isolated that the presence of epistemic modal adverbs (e.g., "likely", "probably") was the primary cause behind DistilBERT's analytical classification degradation.

## 9. Stretch Features & Advanced Analysis

### A. Inter-Annotator Reliability

To mathematically validate the objectivity of our label boundaries, a secondary developer independently annotated a subset of 35 raw text rows from the dataset.

- **Observed Agreement Rate:** 88.57% (31 out of 35 examples matched identically).
- **Cohen's Kappa ($\kappa$):** 0.828 (Indicating "Excellent" or "Almost Perfect" agreement beyond chance).
- **Disagreement Analysis:** The 4 cases of disagreement occurred exclusively on the boundary between `technical_analysis` and `hot_take`. For instance, on a post debating engine wear maps, the secondary annotator flagged it as analysis, while the primary taxonomy ruled it a `hot_take` due to a lack of explicit, quantified telemetry outputs. This confirms that human bias slips in when technical vocabulary is dense but empty of actual mathematical proofs.

### B. Confidence Calibration Analysis

We audited whether DistilBERT’s softmax probability distributions correlate to actual predictive truth (i.e., does a 90% confidence score mean it's right more often than a 60% confidence score?).

- **High Confidence Bin ($\ge 90\%$):** 26 test examples fell here. The model achieved **100% accuracy** on this cohort.
- **Low/Moderate Confidence Bin ($< 90\%$):** 6 test examples fell here (predominantly the borderline technical rants). The model achieved **66.7% accuracy** on this cohort.
- **Conclusion:** The model's confidence is highly calibrated. When it encounters clear structural markers (capitalization for reactions, or flat assertions for hot takes), its certainty peaks and matches reality. When it hits ambiguous boundary sentences, its confidence drops to the 60–70% range, signaling structural uncertainty.

### C. Systematic Error Pattern Analysis

Beyond individual errors, a macro-level evaluation reveals a systematic pattern across the entire error set: **The Epistemic Uncertainty Vulnerability**.
DistilBERT consistently struggles to maintain the boundary between `technical_analysis` and `hot_take` when a post uses complex engineering terminology but softens the claim with speculative modifiers (e.g., _“likely”_, _“potentially”_, _“suggests”_). Because the training set contains numerous speculative `hot_take` entries expressing driver transfer rumors using uncertain terms, the model has falsely generalized that _uncertainty words $\rightarrow$ Hot Take_, over-indexing on single speculative words and blinding itself to the structural telemetry data present in the surrounding text.
